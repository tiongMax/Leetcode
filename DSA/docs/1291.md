# Roadmap: From Math to Window Slicing (LeetCode 1291)

## 📌 The Evolution of the Solution

### Stage 1: The Brute Force (Initial Instinct)
* **The Idea:** Loop through every single integer from `low` to `high` and check if its digits are sequential.
* **The Bottleneck:** If `high = 10^9`, looping $10^9$ times will instantly result in a **Time Limit Exceeded (TLE)** error.

### Stage 2: The Mathematical Approach (Your Blackboard Sketch)
* **The Idea:** You realized brute force was too slow, so you tried to target *only* the right lengths. You calculated $\lfloor \frac{\text{low}}{\text{low}} \rfloor$ and boundaries to try and mathematically jump straight to the numbers.
* **The Problem:** The math gets messy quickly. Handling the transitions between digit lengths, dealing with carries, and writing formulaic loops for variables like `123 -> 234` requires complex nested loops and edge-case handling.

### Stage 3: The Pattern Recognition (The Optimal Solution)
* **The Idea:** Instead of looking at the massive range between `low` and `high`, you look at the properties of the target numbers themselves. They *must* be substrings of a master string: `"123456789"`.
* **The Result:** Bound the outer loop by the **lengths** of `low` and `high`, and use a sliding window to slice the master string. It cuts the runtime down to a constant, tiny number of operations ($O(1)$ time complexity).

---

## 💡 3 Signs You Need to Optimize Using "Generation"

When you find yourself stuck in **Stage 2** (wrestling with complex math/bounds), look out for these three green flags to pivot your strategy:

### 1. The "Strict Pattern" Sign
* **The Rule:** If a problem requires elements to follow a very strict, predictable pattern (e.g., *sequential digits*, *palindromes*, *numbers with all identical digits*), **don't search for them—generate them.**
* **Why:** There are millions of numbers between $100$ and $10^9$, but only 36 of them have sequential digits. It is always faster to generate the 36 valid ones and filter them than to try to find them in a haystack of billions.

### 2. The Tiny Sample Space Sign
* **The Rule:** Ask yourself: *"What is the absolute maximum number of valid answers that could ever exist, regardless of the inputs?"*
* **Why:** For this problem, a sequential number can only be between 2 and 9 digits long. Because the entire global sample space is finite and tiny ($<40$ total possibilities), an $O(1)$ generation approach will always beat an $O(N)$ boundary search.

### 3. The "Master Structure" Hint
* **The Rule:** If you can write out a single sequence or string that contains *every single possible building block* of the answer (like `"123456789"`), the problem is secretly a **Sliding Window** or **String Manipulation** problem, not a math problem.

## 🛠️ The Core Strategy Template
Whenever you see a problem asking you to find numbers matching a strict format within a range `[low, high]`, use this workflow:
1. **Calculate lengths** of bounds to establish window constraints.
2. **Generate candidates** sequentially using a master sequence.
3. **Filter by bounds** and collect the valid outputs.
